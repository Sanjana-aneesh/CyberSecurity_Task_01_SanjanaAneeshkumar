# Part D: Threat Identification

## 1. Insider Threat

**Description:** An insider threat originates from within the organization — current or former employees, students, contractors, or anyone with legitimate access who misuses that access, either maliciously or accidentally, to harm the organization.

**Possible Impact:** Leaked confidential data, sabotaged systems, altered records (e.g., a student or staff member tampering with grades), or financial fraud — often harder to detect than external attacks since the person already has authorized access.

**Prevention Methods:**
- Apply the principle of least privilege — give users only the access they need
- Monitor and log access to sensitive systems and data
- Conduct background checks and security training for staff with sensitive access
- Revoke access immediately when someone leaves the organization

## 2. Malware

**Description:** Malware is malicious software designed to damage, disrupt, or gain unauthorized access to systems — including viruses, worms, trojans, ransomware, and spyware.

**Possible Impact:** Data loss or theft, system downtime, financial loss from ransom payments, and reputational damage if the institution's systems are compromised.

**Prevention Methods:**
- Install and maintain updated antivirus/endpoint protection on all systems
- Keep operating systems and software patched
- Restrict use of unauthorized USB drives and downloads
- Train staff and students to avoid suspicious downloads and email attachments

## 3. Phishing

**Description:** Phishing involves fraudulent emails or messages designed to trick recipients into revealing credentials, clicking malicious links, or installing malware, often by impersonating a trusted source like IT support or college administration.

**Possible Impact:** Stolen login credentials leading to unauthorized access to student/faculty accounts, financial fraud, or a foothold for larger attacks like ransomware.

**Prevention Methods:**
- Conduct regular phishing-awareness training and simulated phishing tests
- Enable Multi-Factor Authentication on all accounts
- Use email filtering tools to catch suspicious messages
- Encourage reporting of suspicious emails to IT

## 4. Weak Passwords

**Description:** Weak, reused, or easily guessable passwords make it significantly easier for attackers to gain unauthorized access to accounts and systems through brute-force or credential-stuffing attacks.

**Possible Impact:** Account takeover, unauthorized access to sensitive systems (student records, exam portals), and potential lateral movement into other connected systems.

**Prevention Methods:**
- Enforce a strong password policy (minimum length, complexity)
- Require MFA wherever possible
- Encourage use of password managers
- Implement account lockout after repeated failed login attempts

## 5. Unpatched Systems

**Description:** Unpatched systems are those running outdated software with known security vulnerabilities that haven't been fixed, making them easy targets for attackers using publicly known exploits.

**Possible Impact:** Exploitation of known vulnerabilities can lead to full system compromise, data breaches, or malware infections (as seen in the WannaCry case, where an unpatched vulnerability led to widespread ransomware infection).

**Prevention Methods:**
- Establish a routine patch management schedule
- Prioritize patching for critical and internet-facing systems
- Retire or isolate legacy systems that no longer receive vendor support
- Use vulnerability scanning tools to identify outdated software
