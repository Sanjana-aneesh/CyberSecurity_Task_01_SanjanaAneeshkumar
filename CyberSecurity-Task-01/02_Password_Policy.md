# Part B: Password Security Review

## What makes a strong password?

A strong password is long, random, and unpredictable. It typically combines uppercase and lowercase letters, numbers, and special characters, and avoids dictionary words, common patterns, or anything tied to personal information (like a name, birthday, or pet). Length matters more than complexity alone — a long passphrase is often stronger and easier to remember than a short, complex string.

## Why should passwords be unique?

If the same password is reused across multiple accounts, a breach on one site can expose every other account that uses the same password. Attackers routinely take leaked credentials from one breach and try them on other popular services — a technique called "credential stuffing." Unique passwords contain the damage to a single account if one service is compromised.

## What is Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)?

MFA is a security method that requires two or more independent forms of verification before granting access to an account — typically something you know (password), something you have (a phone, authenticator app, or security key), or something you are (fingerprint, face scan). Even if an attacker steals your password, MFA blocks access unless they also have the second factor, making accounts significantly harder to compromise.

## What are the risks of password reuse?

- A single leaked password can compromise multiple accounts at once
- Attackers use automated credential-stuffing tools to test reused passwords across many sites
- Sensitive accounts (banking, email) become vulnerable through a breach on an unrelated, less-secure site
- It becomes harder to know which accounts are affected once a breach is discovered, since the same credentials are everywhere

## Password Policy — Best Practices

1. Use at least 12–16 characters, longer where the service allows it
2. Combine uppercase letters, lowercase letters, numbers, and special characters
3. Avoid personal information such as names, birthdays, or pet names
4. Never reuse the same password across multiple accounts
5. Use a password manager to generate and store unique passwords
6. Enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) wherever it's offered
7. Change passwords immediately if a service reports a data breach
8. Avoid common substitution patterns (e.g., "P@ssw0rd123") since these are easily guessed by modern cracking tools
