# Part F: Security Reflection

Going through this personal security audit gave me a clearer, more structured picture of my own digital habits. Overall, I was relieved to find that most of my security basics were already in good shape — my device is fully updated, antivirus (360 Total Security) is active with a clean scan history, and the firewall is enabled across all network profiles. On the account side, the three accounts I reviewed (Google, GitHub, and Instagram) all have Multi-Factor Authentication enabled, strong passwords, and a recovery email configured. That said, doing this audit made me realize I'd never actually verified these settings systematically before — I assumed they were fine rather than checking, which is a risk in itself since settings can silently change or lapse over time.

Even though no major gaps turned up, this exercise highlighted a few improvements I want to keep up going forward. I'm going to start doing a periodic security check-in (roughly every few months) instead of assuming everything stays configured correctly indefinitely. I also want to extend the same level of scrutiny — MFA, strong unique passwords, recovery options — to other accounts beyond the three I reviewed here, since attackers only need one weak link. Adopting a password manager more consistently across all my accounts, rather than just the major ones, is another concrete next step.

Cybersecurity matters enormously in today's world because so much of our personal, financial, and professional life now lives online. A single compromised account can lead to identity theft, financial loss, or exposure of private information, and the consequences often extend beyond the individual to friends, family, or employers whose data may be connected. As more devices and services become interconnected, the attack surface keeps growing, and attackers constantly develop new techniques like phishing and social engineering that exploit human behavior rather than just technical weaknesses. This audit reinforced that good cyber hygiene isn't a one-time setup — it's an ongoing habit, and verifying rather than assuming is just as important as the settings themselves.
