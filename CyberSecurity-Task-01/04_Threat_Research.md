# Part D: Cyber Threat Research

## 1. Phishing

**Definition:** Phishing is a social engineering attack where attackers send fraudulent messages — usually emails, texts, or fake websites — designed to look like they come from a trustworthy source, in order to trick victims into revealing sensitive information like passwords or card details, or into installing malware.

**Real-world Example:** A common example is an email pretending to be from a bank, asking the user to "verify" their account by clicking a link that leads to a fake login page designed to capture credentials.

**Prevention Methods:**
- Check the sender's actual email address, not just the display name
- Hover over links before clicking to verify the real destination URL
- Never enter credentials after clicking a link from an unsolicited email
- Enable MFA so a stolen password alone isn't enough
- Report suspicious emails instead of clicking or replying

## 2. Malware

**Definition:** Malware (malicious software) is any software intentionally designed to damage, disrupt, or gain unauthorized access to a computer system. This includes viruses, worms, trojans, spyware, and adware.

**Real-world Example:** A user downloads a "free" pirated software installer that secretly bundles spyware, which then logs keystrokes and steals saved browser passwords.

**Prevention Methods:**
- Only download software from official, trusted sources
- Keep antivirus software installed and updated
- Avoid clicking on pop-up ads or unknown attachments
- Keep the operating system and applications patched
- Regularly scan the system for threats

## 3. Ransomware

**Definition:** Ransomware is a type of malware that encrypts a victim's files or locks them out of their system, then demands payment (usually cryptocurrency) in exchange for restoring access.

**Real-world Example:** The 2021 Colonial Pipeline attack in the US involved ransomware that forced the company to shut down fuel pipeline operations until a ransom was paid, causing widespread fuel shortages.

**Prevention Methods:**
- Maintain regular, offline backups of important data
- Keep systems and software updated to patch known vulnerabilities
- Avoid opening attachments or links from unknown senders
- Use endpoint protection software with ransomware detection
- Segment networks so an infection can't spread freely

## 4. Social Engineering

**Definition:** Social engineering is the psychological manipulation of people into performing actions or divulging confidential information, exploiting trust, fear, urgency, or authority rather than technical vulnerabilities.

**Real-world Example:** An attacker calls an employee pretending to be from the IT department, claiming there's an urgent issue, and convinces them to share their login credentials over the phone.

**Prevention Methods:**
- Verify identity through a separate, trusted channel before sharing sensitive info
- Be skeptical of urgent or pressuring requests
- Train employees/individuals to recognize manipulation tactics
- Never share passwords or OTPs with anyone, even "support staff"
- Follow organizational verification procedures for sensitive requests

## 5. Data Breach

**Definition:** A data breach is an incident where sensitive, protected, or confidential information is accessed, disclosed, or stolen without authorization — often due to hacking, weak security, or insider threats.

**Real-world Example:** A breach where an unsecured database exposes millions of user records (names, emails, passwords) that are later sold or leaked on the dark web.

**Prevention Methods:**
- Use strong, unique passwords and enable MFA on all accounts
- Encrypt sensitive data both at rest and in transit
- Apply the principle of least privilege for data access
- Regularly audit systems for misconfigurations or vulnerabilities
- Monitor accounts for breach exposure using services like Have I Been Pwned
